# Behavioural Examples (STAR Format)

> These examples are used to prepare for behavioural interview questions and to add
> authentic detail to cover letters. Write each one using the STAR framework:
> **Situation → Task → Action → Result**.
>
> Be specific. Use real numbers, names (where appropriate), and outcomes.
> A strong STAR example is 4–6 sentences, not a paragraph-long essay.

---

## 1. Leadership

<!--
Think of a time you led a team, project, or initiative — even informally.
This doesn't have to be a management role. Leading a workshop, mentoring
a colleague, or driving a decision all count.
-->

**Situation:** [What was the context? Where were you working, and what was happening?]

**Task:** [What were you specifically responsible for or trying to achieve?]

**Action:** [What did you do? Be specific about YOUR contribution, not the team's.]

**Result:** [What happened? Quantify if possible — "reduced by 15%", "delivered 2 weeks early", "adopted by 3 other teams".]

---

## 2. Conflict Resolution

<!--
Describe a time you navigated a disagreement or tension — with a colleague,
stakeholder, or client. Focus on how you handled it constructively.
-->

**Situation:** [What was the conflict or disagreement about?]

**Task:** [What was your role in resolving it?]

**Action:** [How did you approach the conversation or resolution?]

**Result:** [What was the outcome? Did the relationship improve? Was the project unblocked?]

---

## 3. Dealing with Ambiguity

<!--
Think of a time you had to make progress without clear direction, requirements,
or precedent. How did you create clarity for yourself and others?
-->

**Situation:** [What was unclear or undefined?]

**Task:** [What did you need to deliver despite the ambiguity?]

**Action:** [How did you navigate the uncertainty? Did you research, prototype, consult experts?]

**Result:** [What did you deliver, and how was it received?]

---

## 4. Delivering Under Pressure

<!--
Describe a time you met a tight deadline, handled a crisis, or delivered
quality work under difficult circumstances.
-->

**Situation:** [What created the pressure — a deadline, a crisis, resource constraints?]

**Task:** [What specifically needed to be done?]

**Action:** [How did you prioritise, adapt, or push through?]

**Result:** [Did you meet the deadline? What was the quality of the outcome?]

---

## 5. Cross-Functional Collaboration

<!--
Think of a time you worked effectively with people from different teams,
disciplines, or backgrounds. Content designers might work with developers,
product managers, researchers, and subject matter experts — pick an example
that shows you bridge gaps between disciplines.
-->

**Situation:** [Who were the different teams or disciplines involved?]

**Task:** [What were you trying to achieve together?]

**Action:** [How did you communicate, align, or facilitate across the group?]

**Result:** [What was the outcome of the collaboration?]

---

## 6. Failure and Learning

<!--
This is often the most powerful example in an interview. Pick a genuine mistake
or failure — not a humble-brag. Show what you learned and how you changed.
-->

**Situation:** [What went wrong?]

**Task:** [What were you trying to do when it failed?]

**Action:** [What did you do after the failure? How did you respond?]

**Result:** [What did you learn, and how have you applied that lesson since?]
